❓ What is Aplazo?

Aplazo is a Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) platform in Mexico. A customer buys something at a store — say, a $3,000 MXN phone — and instead of paying the full amount, they pay in installments: maybe 6 payments of $500 MXN each, once a month.

Aplazo pays the merchant upfront and then collects those installments from the customer's debit or credit card automatically, every month, until the loan is repaid.

💳 What is a Payment Service Provider (PSP)?

To charge a customer's card, Aplazo doesn't connect directly to Visa or Mastercard. Instead, it works through intermediary companies called PSPs that handle the technical connection to the banking network. Think of them like shipping carriers: they all deliver the same package (the charge), but with different prices and reliability depending on the destination (the bank).

⚠️ The actual problem: static routing

Before this project, Aplazo's system had no intelligence about which PSP to use. Every transaction went to the same PSP by default, regardless of which bank issued the card, whether it's a batch run or user-initiated, whether the error guarantees failure, or how many retries have already happened.

The consequence: Aplazo was sending ~55% of all traffic to psp-o (49.8% AR) while psp-c (73.4% AR, cheaper fee) was underutilized. Money was being left on the table on every billing cycle.

🔍 Key findings from the data — and why each one matters

Each finding below drove a specific design decision in the orchestrator. The "So we built" line is the direct consequence of the data.

Finding 1 — The most important PSP is the one Aplazo uses the least

psp-c has the best approval rate (73.4%) and the lowest fee (1.55%) — it wins on every dimension. But legacy routing sends only 18.8% of traffic there and dumps 54.8% on psp-o, which approves less than half of transactions (49.8%).

In plain terms: Imagine you have four checkout lanes at a supermarket. Lane C is the fastest and cheapest. The current system sends most customers to Lane O, which is slow and expensive. Nobody decided this — it's just how the static rules were written. The orchestrator fixes the default.

So we built: A scoring engine that routes to psp-c first by default, and only uses psp-o and psp-d as fallbacks — not as primary lanes.
Finding 2 — The bank that issued the card matters more than the PSP you choose

The same card issuer can get completely different results depending on which PSP processes it. Nu MX cards: 81.2% approval on psp-c but only 15.8% on psp-e. Banco Mercantil del Norte cards: 60.7% on psp-c but 68.5% on psp-e — psp-e actually wins here.

In plain terms: It's like knowing that Airline A is great for domestic flights but always delays internationals, while Airline B is the opposite. The right choice depends on your destination — not just which airline is "better overall." The card's bank is the destination.

So we built: An issuer × PSP matrix. For every transaction where we know the card's bank, we route to the PSP with the highest historical approval rate for that specific bank — not the highest average overall.
Finding 3 — Overnight batch payments are a completely different problem

47% of all transactions run automatically at night (called "cron batch"). Their approval rate is 30.4% — vs. 78.8% when a user is actively at checkout. psp-e is especially bad for these: 29–35% approval on batch.

In plain terms: When a customer is at checkout, they can fix problems in real time — change cards, top up their account, call their bank. At 3am when the batch runs, nobody is watching. If the card fails, it just fails. Retrying 5 times at 3am on a card that drained at midnight is pure waste.

So we built: A separate rule set for cron transactions — max 3 attempts instead of 5, psp-e is excluded entirely, and the retry window is tighter. Cron and checkout are not the same product.
Finding 4 — Switching payment processor on retry does absolutely nothing

We analyzed over 500,000 retry attempts. Same PSP retry: 50.6% approval. Switched to a different PSP: 50.5% approval. The difference is 0.1 percentage points — statistically zero.

In plain terms: The whole industry assumes "if PSP A fails, try PSP B." The data says that's wrong. Whether a payment succeeds on retry depends on the type of error, not which company processes the retry. A declined card is a declined card — it doesn't matter who declines it the second time.

So we built: A system focused entirely on getting the first routing decision right, not on building smarter cascade logic. Retry is a safety net, not a strategy.
Finding 5 — Nearly a million failures per month have zero chance of ever succeeding

771,000 transactions per month fail with "insufficient funds" and show 0% recovery rate on the next attempt. The system was retrying them anyway — paying a transaction fee each time.

In plain terms: If someone's bank account is empty, trying to charge the card again in the next hour will not fill the account. Every retry costs Aplazo a fee. We were paying to confirm what we already knew.

So we built: A hard stop list. If the error message signals a permanent problem — empty account, stolen card, fraud flag — the orchestrator stops immediately and does not try again. No fee wasted. This is the single highest-ROI rule in the entire system.
Finding 6 — Manual agent retries are a behavioral problem, not a routing problem

The admin-dashboard channel — where Aplazo agents manually trigger payment retries — has only 8.2% approval rate. The top error is "insufficient funds" — repeated, over and over.

In plain terms: Agents are retrying cards belonging to customers who are already 2–3 payments behind. Those customers have either no money in the account or have blocked the card. No PSP will approve it. The problem is not which processor the agent uses — the problem is that a card is the wrong payment channel for this customer at this moment.

So we built (v1): The hard decline interceptor also covers agent retries — it stops the loop. What's coming (v1.5): After 2 agent-dashboard failures on the same card, automatically send the customer a SPEI payment link via WhatsApp. That reaches a different funding source entirely and breaks the dead-card loop.

⚡ How the PaymentOrchestrator class works

The orchestrator takes one transaction as input and returns one decision as output. It runs three checks in sequence:

1
Hard Decline Check
Is the error one that will never recover? ("insufficient funds", "fraud detected", "card expired"). If yes → stop immediately, no fee wasted.
2
Retry Cap Check
Has this installment been attempted too many times? Normal: max 5. Cron: max 3. Critically low issuers (Bradescard, Liverpool): max 2.
3
PSP Scoring
Score every PSP using a weighted blend of cost efficiency and issuer-specific approval rate. The blend ratio is controlled by The Knob.

🎛️ The Knob — the core control parameter

A single number between 0.0 and 1.0 that controls what the orchestrator optimizes for:

0.0 — Minimize cost
Route to cheapest PSP (psp-c 1.55%) 0.5
Balanced (recommended) 1.0 — Maximize acceptance
Route by issuer-PSP performance matrix
Live example — Banco Mercantil del Norte, $870 MXN:
knob=1.0 → psp-e (68.5% AR, fee $14.62 MXN)
knob=0.0 → psp-c (60.7% AR, fee $13.48 MXN)

🛡️ Volume Guardrail

Even at knob=0.0, the orchestrator will not send 100% of traffic to psp-c. A minimum percentage (default: 10%) is reserved for secondary PSPs so they stay operationally active and Aplazo avoids a single point of failure.

🚫 What v1 intentionally does NOT do — and the exact reason why

These are not oversights. Each one was a deliberate decision. If the panel asks "why didn't you build X," the answer is here.

❌ No machine learning model

Why we didn't build it: To train an ML model that predicts "which PSP should I use for this transaction?", you need examples where you tried the same transaction on different PSPs and saw both outcomes. That data does not exist. The March dataset only shows what happened on the PSP that was actually used. If we train ML on that, it learns "psp-c is always great" — because psp-c always got the good transactions routed to it. That's not intelligence, that's a circular loop.

What unlocks it: Six months of A/B routing experiments — where we deliberately send 10% of traffic to random PSP assignment — generates the counterfactual data needed to train properly.

Roadmap: v3 — after A/B experiment data is collected. The rules-based system generates the training labels that ML needs.
❌ No issuer routing for 62% of transactions

Why we can't do it: psp-o and psp-d — which together handle 62% of transaction volume — do not include the card's BIN number in their event data. The BIN is the first 6–8 digits of a card number that tells you which bank issued it. Without it, we cannot look up the issuer, and without the issuer, we cannot apply the issuer × PSP matrix. We route those transactions generically, exactly as today's static system does. No regression — just no improvement yet.

What unlocks it: A one-time data pipeline fix — ask psp-o and psp-d to include BIN in their webhook payloads. This is the single highest-leverage infrastructure change available. Every day without it, 62% of volume routes blind.

Roadmap: v2 priority #1 — before any ML work, before any new PSP integrations.
❌ No real-time approval rate updates

Why we didn't build it: The issuer × PSP matrix is refreshed monthly from the analysis scripts. If a PSP starts degrading on a Tuesday afternoon, the orchestrator won't reflect it until next month's refresh. Building a real-time metrics pipeline — streaming event processing, a metrics store, hourly recalculation — is significant infrastructure that would delay shipping v1 by weeks. The Δ Margin alert in the Observability layer catches degradation within 4 hours, which is good enough for v1.

What unlocks it: A production metrics store connected to the live event stream. The analysis script logic already exists — it just needs to run hourly instead of monthly.

Roadmap: v1.5 — the first infrastructure upgrade after v1 is stable in production.
❌ No SPEI / WhatsApp fallback channel

Why we didn't build it: The brief scope is card transactions. SPEI is a bank transfer — a different payment rail entirely. Integrating it requires a separate API connection, different error handling, different reconciliation. Scoping it into v1 would have made the card routing worse by splitting engineering focus. But the data strongly justifies it: admin-dashboard has 8.2% approval because agents are retrying dead cards. After 2 failures, the card is not the right instrument — SPEI or a WhatsApp payment link is.

Roadmap: v1.5 — highest-impact product change for late-stage collection. Doesn't require any PSP routing changes, just a channel switch trigger.
❌ No customer-level memory across installments

Why we didn't build it: The orchestrator sees each transaction in isolation. It does not know that this is the same customer's 3rd failed installment in a row, or that they have 4 more installments due. That context is in loans_data-march2026.csv, which we analyzed but did not wire into the routing engine for v1. Building it requires joining two data sources at request time, which adds latency and complexity. The routing rules work correctly without it — they just can't prioritize "this customer always pays eventually" over "this customer has never paid."

Roadmap: v2 — a lightweight customer reliability score from payment history. All the data is already in our pipeline. This turns the orchestrator from transaction-level intelligence to customer-level intelligence.

🔄 1. PSP Merchant Score Degradation — The Decline Spiral

Silent risk not visible in the data

Every declined transaction Aplazo submits to a PSP is logged by that PSP's internal fraud engine. If our ratio of declines-to-approvals on a given PSP crosses an internal threshold, the PSP's risk system starts pre-declining transactions it would otherwise approve — treating Aplazo as a risky merchant. Our orchestrator has no concept of its own submission quality per PSP. It could be actively degrading psp-c's future performance by routing too many hard declines through it before the interceptor catches them.

Fix for v1.5: Track a submission health ratio per PSP: approvals / total submissions on a rolling 24-hour window. If it drops below a threshold, reduce volume to that PSP until it recovers — even if the scoring formula says otherwise.
What to say: "The orchestrator can't just be a greedy algorithm chasing the highest approval rate. It needs to protect the transaction pipeline quality of its best providers. A silently degraded psp-c is worse than a slightly lower-AR psp-e."

📊 2. We Have loans_data and Aren't Using It

Data asset going to waste

The challenge handed us loans_data with loan status (DEFAULTED, active) and installment numbers. We never join it to the routing logic. Two immediate wins this unlocks:

  • Zombie Account Elimination: If a loan is already marked DEFAULTED in our database, the orchestrator should automatically kill all retry loops. We are burning PSP submission overhead on accounts Aplazo has already written off.
  • Installment Priority Scoring: Installment #1 on a new loan has high behavioral value (sets the repayment habit). Installment #8 on a loan already 3 months late has near-zero collection probability. The retry cap and routing aggressiveness should scale with installment strategic value, not be uniform.
Fix for v1.5: JOIN payment_events on loan_id to bring in loan status and installment position as routing inputs. Zero new data collection required — it's already in our files.

🔐 3. The 3DS Cron Veil — Authentication Failures Masking as Declines

Hypothesis backed by cron AR pattern

For certain issuers and amounts above a threshold, Visa/Mastercard requires 3DS authentication — an SMS code or in-app biometric confirmation. In a cron batch, no user is present to complete that step. Those transactions fail at the authentication layer, not the authorization layer — and they show up in our data as generic bank declines. We are counting them as PSP failures when they are actually a channel mismatch.

This means our cron AR of 30.4% may be artificially suppressed by 3DS failures that no PSP change can fix.

Fix for v1.5: Identify issuer + amount bands that consistently trigger 3DS (BBVA credit above ~$2,000 MXN is a common profile in Mexico). Exclude these from cron routing entirely. Flag them for user-present re-initiation via push notification. Don't keep hammering a gate that requires a human to open it.
What to say: "Our data shows cron converts at 30.4%. The assumption is that this is a processor issue. My hypothesis is that a significant portion of these failures are invisible 3DS authentication timeouts — a channel problem, not a PSP problem. That's a fundamentally different fix."

📅 4. March Data Has Semana Santa — Possible Seasonal Overfit

Analytical honesty the panel will respect

Our entire routing model is calibrated on one month: March 2026. March contains Semana Santa — the largest consumer spending and travel period in Mexico. Cards are used differently: higher balances drawn down, more international transactions, different bank fraud patterns. The issuer × PSP approval rates we are using as routing rules may be specific to Easter-week behavior.

What this means: A routing model that works well in March may underperform in January (post-Christmas debt hangover), September (back-to-school spending), or December (holiday peak). Before production rollout, validate the issuer-PSP performance matrix against at least 3 non-adjacent, operationally normal months.
What to say: "We must acknowledge a core analytical boundary: this data is one month, and it's not a representative month. Before we push this live, we validate against non-holiday months to confirm the routing rules are structurally stable — not Semana Santa artifacts."

💳 5. Card Token Staleness — "Invalid Card" ≠ Customer Won't Pay

User intent hidden behind an error code

When the orchestrator sees "invalid card or card type," it correctly stops retrying — recovery rate is 0% for that token. But a senior PM asks: why is the card invalid? In many cases, it's not that the customer is unwilling to pay. It's that they received a replacement card (expired, lost, bank-issued fraud replacement) and their old token stored in Aplazo's vault is stale. A perfectly willing customer with a new valid card is being permanently abandoned.

Fix for v1.5: When "invalid card" fires on an installment that previously succeeded (the customer did pay before), trigger a re-tokenization request via WhatsApp or push notification — not a retry on the dead token. The action is "update your card details," not "try the charge again." Zero PSP cost, potential full recovery.

👥 6. Multi-Installment Memory — We Treat Each Attempt in Isolation

Cross-installment signal available in our data

The orchestrator currently treats every payment attempt as an independent event. But consumer credit risk is highly correlated across installments. If a customer fails installment #3, they are very likely to fail installment #4 on the same card. We never join on customer_id across installments to share the signal.

  • Customer with 2+ consecutive cron failures → downgrade all remaining installments to "user-present only, no cron" and trigger a proactive WhatsApp outreach
  • Customer with consistent on-time history → allow higher retry cap (lower risk, proven behavior)
Fix for v2: Build a lightweight customer reliability score from payment_events grouped by customer_id. All inputs are already in our data. This is a cross-installment memory layer that makes the orchestrator context-aware at the customer level, not just the transaction level.

In a healthy system it should receive the minimum guardrail floor (e.g., 5–10%) just to keep the connection alive — and nothing more.

💡 Why do the names end in letters (-c, -e, -o, -d)?

These are Aplazo's anonymized internal codenames for their payment processor partners. The challenge data redacts the real company names intentionally — all candidates work with the same anonymized dataset so no one has an unfair advantage from knowing a PSP's real identity.

The letters themselves have no inherent meaning (they're not abbreviations of company names). What matters is their performance profile: their fee rate, their approval rate by issuer and card type, and whether they log BIN data. That is everything you need to route intelligently.

💼 Product Strategy & The Knob

1
"How did you arrive at your definition of 'Value' for this orchestrator?"
Core PM judgment
📐 Open: Scoring Formula tab
Value is not just a high approval rate, and it's not just low fees — those are opposing forces. I defined value as Net Recovery Margin: the expected revenue we collect from an installment after subtracting processing fees, weighted by the probability the transaction actually succeeds. The scoring formula makes this concrete: Score = (knob × AR) + (1−knob × cost efficiency). This means every routing decision is a mathematical trade-off on the cost/acceptance frontier, not a gut call.
💡 Point to the formula on screen. Say "this is not a black box — every decision is traceable to this equation."
2
"If The Knob is at 0.5, how does the system choose between a PSP with higher fees but better approval rates?"
Scoring logic
📐 Open: Scoring Formula tab → show the fee reference table
At 0.5 the formula weighs cost and AR equally. Let's take a real example: for a Banco Mercantil del Norte card, psp-e has a 68.5% approval rate vs. psp-c's 60.7% — a 7.8 percentage point advantage. But psp-e charges 1.68% vs psp-c's 1.55% — 8.4% more expensive in relative terms. At knob=0.5 the AR advantage outweighs the cost penalty, so psp-e wins. Switch to knob=0.0 and psp-c wins because cost dominates. You can see this live in the Simulator right now.

"What happens if the finance team slides The Knob all the way to 0.0?"
Operator controls
⚡ Open: Simulator tab → set knob to 0.0
At 0.0, the AR term drops to zero in the formula. The engine ignores approval rate differences entirely and routes everything to psp-c — our cheapest processor at 1.55%. This mode makes sense during aggressive cost-cutting periods or when margins are tight — but it sacrifices collection revenue. "What if PSP-C goes down completely during peak hours? What does your system do?"
Resilience & failure modes

Two protections. First, the Volume Guardrail — I set a minimum traffic floor per secondary PSP (default 10%), which means psp-o and psp-e are always receiving some traffic. Their connections stay warm, not cold. If psp-c goes down, the operator slides the guardrail to 0% for psp-c and raises it for the others — no code deploy, no engineering ticket, recovers in under 2 minutes. Second, the Δ Margin alert fires automatically when psp-c's real-time AR drops to zero, flagging the outage before the operator even notices it manually.

📊 Data Depth & Technical Questions

5
"What was the most non-obvious pattern you found in the data?"
Analytical depth
📊 Open: Data Findings tab → Finding 4 (Retry myth) + Finding 2 (Credit/Debit)
Two things surprised me. First: switching PSPs on retry does nothing. Same PSP retry: 50.6% AR. Switched PSP: 50.5% AR. Statistically identical across half a million retry attempts. The whole industry assumption that "try a different rail" rescues a declined card is not supported by this data. What matters is the error type, not the PSP. Second: on psp-c, debit cards approve better than credit cards (76.7% vs 62.4%). That's counterintuitive — credit cards usually have higher limits and more fraud protection. It suggests Aplazo's debit card base includes banks with pre-authorized BNPL agreements, while credit cards face more fraud-check friction.
6
"Why does cron get separate logic? Why not treat it like any other payment?"
Cron handling
📊 Open: Data Findings tab → Finding 3 (Cron gateway)
Cron is fundamentally different from user-initiated payments in three ways. One: no user is present — if the card fails, nobody can fix it in the moment. There's no "try another card" option. Two: latency is irrelevant — the user is asleep at 3am, so whether the transaction takes 200ms or 2 seconds changes nothing. Three: the AR profile is completely different — cron has a 30.4% baseline AR vs. 78.8% for checkout. psp-e is especially bad on cron (29–35% AR). So the orchestrator runs cron on a restricted set of PSPs (psp-c and psp-o only), with a tighter retry cap of 3 instead of 5, to stop bleeding fee overhead on dead accounts.
7
"You said psp-o and psp-d don't log BIN data. How is that even possible, and what does it mean for routing?"
Data quality
📊 Open: Data Findings tab → Data Quality Issues table
It means those PSPs don't pass the card BIN back in their event webhook payloads — or they strip it before logging. This is a data pipeline gap on their side, not ours. The practical consequence is severe: 62% of our transaction volume flows through PSPs where we have zero card-level intelligence. We know the amount, the outcome, the gateway — but not which bank issued the card. So the orchestrator cannot apply issuer routing to psp-o or psp-d. They get treated as generic fallbacks ranked only by their global average AR. Fixing this — getting psp-o and psp-d to log BIN data — is the single highest-leverage infrastructure change for v2. It would unlock issuer routing for the majority of our volume.
💡 Frame this as a finding you surfaced, not a flaw you didn't fix. "I identified this gap and it's explicitly in our v2 roadmap."

🛡️ Guardrails, Monitoring & Failure Modes

9
"What is your biggest fear about deploying this v1, and how do you protect against it?"
Senior judgment
🗺️ Product Story tab → Failure Modes table + 🔭 Observability tab
Volume concentration. If the scoring math works perfectly, 100% of traffic goes to psp-c. That sounds great until psp-c has a 2am outage and our backup PSPs haven't processed a real transaction in weeks — their connections to the banking rails go cold. We also may have contractual volume minimums with psp-o that we'd be violating. The Volume Guardrail is the direct protection: a hard floor that ensures secondary PSPs always receive a minimum share of traffic. The Δ Margin alert is the detection layer: if psp-c goes down and our collection revenue drops below baseline on a 4-hour window, we automatically revert to static routing while the team investigates.
10
"When would your orchestrator make a worse decision than today's static routing?"
Honest trade-offs
🗺️ Product Story tab → Failure Modes table
Three scenarios. One: stale AR matrix. If a PSP degrades after our last monthly data refresh, the orchestrator still routes to it as if it were healthy. The mitigation is the real-time Δ Margin alert — we catch it within 4 hours, not next month. Two: new issuers with no history. A card from a bank not in our BIN table gets no issuer routing — it falls back to psp-c by default, which is still better than random routing but not optimal. Three: the psp-c × cron artifact. Our data shows 100% approval rate for psp-c on cron, which is almost certainly pre-auth captures being counted as approvals. If we trust that number, we'd over-route cron to psp-c. We flagged this and need engineering to confirm the transaction type before production rollout.
💡 Saying this out loud shows senior honesty. The panel respects that you know where your own system breaks.
11
"You have a hard decline interceptor. How do you know 'insufficient funds' will never recover?"
Data validation
📊 Data Findings tab → Retry Analysis section
The data is unambiguous. Across 518,075 attempts with the error "the card doesn't have sufficient funds," the next-attempt recovery rate is exactly 0.0%. Same for fraud risk detected: 93,023 occurrences, 0% recovery. I validated this across all variants of the string — "insufficient funds," "INSUFFICIENT_FUNDS," "fondos insuficientes" — because the data has case and language inconsistencies. After normalizing, every variant resolves to the same 0% recovery pattern. The economic logic is also straightforward: if someone doesn't have money in their account right now, retrying the same card 10 minutes later won't change their account balance. The only scenario where it would recover is if they topped up their account between attempts — which happens rarely enough that the retry cost exceeds the expected recovery value.

🗺️ Product Narrative & Roadmap

12
"Why rules-based v1 instead of jumping straight to ML?"
Build strategy
🗺️ Product Story tab → Configuration Model section
Three reasons. First: trust. An ML model is a black box to the payments ops team. If it makes a wrong decision at 2am, they can't audit it. A rules-based system is fully transparent — every decision traces back to a formula and a data observation. Trust has to come before complexity. Second: data for counterfactuals. To train an ML model that predicts "what would psp-e's AR be for this transaction if we had routed it there instead of psp-c?", we need split routing experiments. We don't have that data yet. Third: the rules already capture most of the signal. The issuer × PSP matrix explains the majority of approval rate variance. An ML model that learns what the matrix already knows isn't worth the operational complexity in v1. ML is explicitly on the v2 roadmap — after we run A/B experiments and collect the counterfactual data needed to train it properly.

"You mentioned admin-dashboard has only 8.2% AR. What do you do about that?"
Product intuition
📊 Data Findings tab → Finding 3 (Gateway table)
The 8.2% AR on admin-dashboard isn't a PSP problem — it's a behavioral pattern. Agents are manually retrying cards belonging to customers who are already 2–3 installments behind. That's a v1.5 feature that could meaningfully move the needle on late-stage collection without any PSP routing changes at all.
